40 Meta-specification
41 ------------------
42 
43 The previous coursework was about deep diving on one
44 problem, and (hopefully) trying out a number of alternative
45 strategies. This coursework represents the other end
46 of the spectrum, which is sadly the more common end: you
47 haven't got much time, either to analyse the problem or
48 to do low-level optimisation, and the problem is actually
49 a large number of sub-problems. So the goal here is to
50 identify and capture as much of the low-hanging performance
51 fruit as possible while not breaking anything.
52 
53 The code-base I've given you is somewhat baroque,
54 (though not as convoluted as my original version,
55 I took pity), and despite having some rather iffy
56 OOP practises, actually has things quite reasonably
57 isolated. You will probably encounter the problem
58 that sometimes the reference solution starts to take
59 a very long time at large scales, but the persistence
60 framework gives you a way of dealing with that.
61 
62 Beyond that, there isn't a lot more guidance, either
63 in terms of what you should focus on, or how
64 _exactly_ it will be measured. Part of the assesment
65 is in seeing whether you can work out what can be
66 accelerated, and where you should spend your time.
67 And in reacting to externally evolving specs and
68 code - the Friday 13th comment is true, though the
69 change is minor (there aren't another five problems),
70 additive (all work done this week is needed and
71 evaluated for the final assesment), and has a default
72 fallback (a git pull will bring any submission back
73 into correcness).

92 Deliverable format
93 ------------------
94 
95 - As before, your repository should contain a readme.txt, readme.pdf, or readme.md covering:
96 
97  - What is the approach used to improve performance, in terms of algorithms, patterns, and optimisations.
98 
99  - A description of any testing methodology or verification.
100 
101  - A summary of how work was partitioned within the pair, including planning, analysis, design, and testing, as well as coding.
102 
103 - Anything in the `include` directory is not owned by you, and subject to change
104 
105  - Changes will happen in an additive way (existing classes and APIs will remain, new ones may be added)
106 
107  - Bug-fixes to `include` stuff are still welcome.
108 
109 - The public entry point to your code is via `puzzler::PuzzleRegistrar::UserRegisterPuzzles`,
110  which must be compiled into the static library `lib/libpuzzler.a`.
111 
112  - Clients will not directly include your code, they will only `#include "puzzler/puzzles.h`,
113  then access puzzles via the registrar. They will get access to the registrar implementation
114  by linking against `lib/libpuzzler.a`.
115 
116  - **Note**: If you do something complicated in your building of libpuzzler, it should still be
117  possible to build it by going into `lib` and calling `make all`.
118 
119 - The programs in `src` have no special meaning or status, they are just example programs
120 
121 The reason for all this strange indirection is that I want to give
122 maximum freedom for you to do strange things within your implementation
123 (example definitions of "strange" include CMake) while still having a clean
124 abstraction layer between your code and the client code.

126 Notes
127 -----
128 
129 All the algorithms here are quite classic, though for the most
130 part different enough to require some thought. Where it is possible
131 to directly use an off-the-shelf implementation (partially true in
132 most cases), you need to bear in mind that you're trying to
133 show-case your understanding and ability here. So if you're
134 relying on someone elses library, you need to:
135 
136 - Correctly and clearly attribute it
137 - Be able to demonstrate you understand how and why it works
138 
139 Make sure you spend a little bit of time thinking about how
140 feasible it is to accelerate something - in some cases you
141 may be able to get linear speed-up in the processor count,
142 in others less so. Sometimes the fundamental algorithmic
143 complexity doesn't look friendly, and can be improved in
144 simple ways.
